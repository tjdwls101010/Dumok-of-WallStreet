# SEPA Framework & Probability Convergence

## WHY — Values & Principles

### The Power of Convergence (V2)

SEPA's edge is not any single filter — it is the requirement that ALL dimensions align simultaneously. Think of four cars arriving at a four-way intersection at the same moment: Trend, Fundamentals, Catalyst, Entry Timing, and Risk Parameters must all converge before action.

When any element disagrees, the verdict is WAIT, never ACT. The collective value of these parameters is greater than the sum of their parts. Each additional confirming factor multiplies probability — this is fundamentally Bayesian thinking applied to stock selection.

A stock may have perfect earnings acceleration but lack a proper VCP base. Another may have textbook VCP formation but deteriorating fundamentals. Neither qualifies. Only full convergence does.

### Strength Begets Strength (V3)

True contrarianism is not buying what's falling — it's having the courage to buy what's rising when everyone thinks it's "too high." Most investors cannot bring themselves to buy stocks at 52-week highs with high P/Es. This psychological barrier IS the edge.

Relative strength, earnings momentum, new highs, institutional accumulation — these are not signs of being "too late" but evidence of emerging superperformance. The comfort of buying weakness is the enemy of superperformance.

When a stock trades at P/E 35x with EPS growing 45%, PEG is 0.78 — the "expensive" stock is actually cheap. The "bargain" at P/E 10x with flat earnings is the real trap. Whole Foods at P/E 30x outperformed Wild Oats at low P/E by orders of magnitude. The market leader's premium reflects its ability to compound earnings.

### History Repeats (V5)

SEPA is built on reverse factor modeling — studying what ACTUALLY happened to superperformance stocks across decades, not what SHOULD happen theoretically. The characteristics that drove superperformance in the 1920s, 1960s, 1990s, and 2010s are remarkably consistent: strong earnings, price trend, institutional accumulation, proper base formation.

Price action reveals truth that opinions cannot. When a stock makes new highs on heavy volume, institutions are voting with capital. When it pulls back on light volume, sellers are exhausted. These patterns repeat because human psychology — greed, fear, hope, ignorance — never changes.

## HOW — Interpretation Methodology

### Five-Element Convergence Assessment

When interpreting pipeline output, evaluate each SEPA element and its convergence state:

**Element 1 — Trend**: The non-negotiable foundation. The Trend Template is the minimum evidence that institutional capital is interested. Without the footprint of institutional money (V2), no company — regardless of fundamentals — can sustain superperformance. A stock trading below its declining 200-day MA has zero institutional support worth following.

**Element 2 — Fundamentals**: Sustainable earnings acceleration is the engine of stock price. But the MECHANISM matters more than the result: revenue-driven growth compounds because markets are large; cost-cutting walks on short legs because costs have a floor. When EPS, Sales, and Margins all accelerate simultaneously (Code 33), it proves the business engine is structurally healthy, not temporarily juiced.

**Element 3 — Catalyst**: Every superperformance stock has a catalyst — a new product, industry shift, FDA approval, or management change that attracts institutional attention. The catalyst may not be obvious at first glance, but detective work on the company's story reveals what could ignite the next move.

**Element 4 — Entry Point**: The VCP is not just a chart pattern — it is a structural mechanism that PROVES supply has been absorbed. Each successive contraction demonstrates fewer sellers remain. Volume drying up near the pivot confirms the selling vacuum. This structure enables tight stops, creating the asymmetric risk-reward architecture (V1) that makes the system work even at 50% win rate.

**Element 5 — Exit Point**: Every position must have a predetermined exit plan before entry. The stop-loss is absolute. The VCP structure that enabled the tight entry also defines the logical stop — just below the last contraction's low.

### Stage Analysis Interpretation

Stage 2 is the ONLY stage to buy. This is not a suggestion — it is a non-negotiable rule rooted in the lifecycle imperative (V4):

- **Stage 1** (Consolidation): The company may have potential, but no institutional interest is visible yet. Patience, not action.
- **Stage 2** (Advancing): Institutional accumulation in progress. MAs aligning bullishly. This is when the fundamental lifecycle (accelerating earnings) aligns with the technical lifecycle (emerging uptrend). The ONLY time to buy.
- **Stage 3** (Topping): Distribution replacing accumulation. Even if fundamentals still look good on paper, the transition from V2 to V3 of the supply/demand curve has begun. Sell or reduce.
- **Stage 4** (Declining): Never buy. No matter how "cheap" the stock looks. Capital preservation (V1) demands avoiding falling knives.

**Stage 1→2 Transition**: The most valuable signal. Watch for: price breaking above flattening 200MA, golden cross (50MA > 200MA), higher highs and higher lows emerging, volume pattern shifting from neutral to accumulation. These transitions often happen while news is still negative — which is why most investors miss them.

### VCP Interpretation

Contraction quality matters more than contraction count. Evaluate:

- **Contraction ratio**: Each successive correction should be ~50% of the prior (ideal). Ratios > 0.7 suggest supply isn't drying up fast enough. Expanding contractions (ratio > 1.0) = distribution, not accumulation.
- **Volume behavior across contractions**: Declining average volume = supply exhaustion. Rising volume across contractions = distribution. This is the single most important VCP quality indicator.
- **Pivot tightness**: A tight pivot (low ATR ratio + low volume percentile near pivot) = highest reliability breakout. Wide, loose pivots are more failure-prone.
- **Time symmetry**: The right side of the base should be constructive — neither too fast (V-shape, suggests fragile recovery) nor too slow (suggests persistent supply).
- **Shakeout quality**: A constructive shakeout (quick undercut + volume surge recovery) near the pivot is actually POSITIVE — it traps weak holders and clears supply before the real move.

### Company Categories

Not all stocks deserve equal attention. When evaluating a company, determine its category:

- **Market Leader**: #1-3 in industry by sales/earnings, gaining market share. These are where most superperformance profits come from. They look "too expensive" — that's the edge. The main questions: What is the competitive advantage? Is the model scalable?
- **Top Competitor**: #2-3 riding the leader's draft. Can take market share and claim the top spot. Track the top 2-3 stocks in every leading group.
- **Category Killer**: Brand and market position so dominant that unlimited capital cannot compete (eBay in auctions, Apple in premium devices). Extremely rare and extremely powerful.
- **Cookie Cutter**: Scalable repeatable model (McDonald's, Starbucks, Costco). Look for same-store sales growth of 10%+, proven in diverse geographies, not over-expanding (>100 stores/year is a warning).
- **Turnaround**: Requires 2+ quarters of 100%+ earnings growth to prove the turn is real. Are margins recovering to prior peak? Is growth from revenue, not just cost-cutting?
- **Laggard**: Cheap P/E is a trap. Stay away. There's always a reason for the low multiple.

### P/E Interpretation

P/E ratios by themselves are "among the most useless statistics on Wall Street." What matters:

- **P/E DIRECTION** is more important than P/E level. Expanding P/E during growth acceleration = market rewarding expected continuation. Contracting P/E = first signal of growth deceleration.
- **High P/E + High Growth** = potentially cheap (PEG < 1.0)
- **Low P/E + Low Growth** = potentially expensive (the "value trap")
- **Cyclical stocks have INVERSE P/E** cycles: high P/E near the bottom (low earnings), low P/E near the top (peak earnings). Applying growth investor P/E logic to cyclicals leads to buying tops and selling bottoms.

### Bottom-Up Sector Leadership

Do not start with industry analysis. Start with individual stocks. The strongest stocks in the leading groups advance BEFORE it's obvious that the group or sector is hot. Individual stocks point you to the group, not the other way around.

Follow the 52-week new high list. Industry groups with a healthy number of stocks hitting new highs early in a bull market will often be the leaders. Your portfolio should consist of the best companies in the top 4-5 sectors.

When a key leader in an industry group breaks down after a big advance, the entire group may follow — like a virus spreading from the leader to the group.

### New Technologies Lifecycle

Every innovation follows a path: emergence → penetration → saturation → decline. When the number of companies in an industry peaks and begins declining (as happened with automobiles in the 1920s, televisions in the 1950s, PCs in the 1980s), the industry's growth phase is ending. Individual companies may still win market share, but the rising-tide tailwind is gone.

## WHEN — Score Caveats & Cross-Field Conflicts

### Cross-Field Conflict Matrix

When pipeline signals conflict, use these interpretation priorities:

**TT 8/8 pass BUT VCP shows distribution pattern** → VCP takes priority. The Trend Template reflects PAST MA alignment; VCP reflects CURRENT supply/demand dynamics. A distribution pattern means supply is increasing — possible Stage 3 transition even while MAs are still bullish. Reduce conviction. (V4 Lifecycle)

**Code 33 PASS BUT RS < 70** → RS warning takes priority for timing. Earnings are accelerating but institutions aren't buying yet. Two possible interpretations: (a) Undiscovered — potential opportunity if catalyst exists. Investigate the story. (b) Institutions see something you don't — hidden risk. Demand additional evidence before entry. Do NOT enter on fundamentals alone without institutional confirmation. (V2 Convergence)

**VCP prime setup BUT market environment is bear** → Market environment takes priority. 90%+ of superperformers emerge during bull markets. VCP breakout success rates collapse in bear environments. Either wait for market to turn or reduce position size by 50%+. Capital preservation overrides setup quality. (V1 Capital Preservation)

**Stage 2 confirmed BUT 3rd base or later** → Base count warning. Late-stage bases (3rd+) have significantly higher failure rates. 1st and 2nd bases in a Stage 2 advance offer the best probability. For 3rd+ bases, reduce position size and tighten stops. (V4 Lifecycle)

**Earnings surprise positive BUT stock price declines** → Price reaction takes priority. "If the stock doesn't respond to good news, something is wrong." Apply the 3 Reactions Test: (1) Initial response — did it rally or sell off? (2) Subsequent resistance — did it hold gains? (3) Resilience — did it recover from pullbacks? Failure on any of these = the surprise was already priced in or worse is coming. (V5 Empirical)

**High SEPA score BUT active sell signals** → V1 overrides all. No analysis, no matter how compelling, justifies ignoring sell signals. Active sell signals (20MA breach, high-volume reversal, climax top) trigger risk management protocol regardless of fundamental or technical score.

**RS 90+ AND P/E 50x** → This is V3 in action. High RS + high P/E is NOT "too expensive." It means institutions are pricing in future growth that justifies the current multiple. Validate with PEG ratio and Code 33 status. If Code 33 confirms, the stock is likely CHEAP on forward estimates. (V3 Strength)

### Screening Strategy

Do NOT use a single screen with 12 criteria — a stock missing ONE by a hair gets filtered out despite meeting the other 11. Instead, run separate smaller screens:

- **Technical screen**: Trend Template pass + RS ≥ 70 + Stage 2
- **Fundamental screen**: Code 33 or EPS acceleration + margin expansion
- **Volume screen**: Accumulation grade A/B + low distribution

Stocks appearing on MULTIPLE screens are the highest-conviction candidates. This multi-screen convergence mirrors the broader SEPA convergence principle.
