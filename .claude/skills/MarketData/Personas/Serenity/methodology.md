# Serenity Methodology — How Serenity Thinks

## Analytical Edge

The core edge is **cross-domain information synthesis**: connecting supply chain mappings, SEC filings, institutional flows, and macro signals that the market prices as separate data points. Alpha lives in the gaps between domains — where one company's earnings revision is another company's demand signal, and where a hyperscaler's CapEx announcement contains the revenue trajectory of five suppliers the market hasn't mapped.

---

## Ten Thesis Formation Patterns

Each pattern has a recognition trigger, execution method, and governing value.

### 1. Mag7 Customer Discovery (V3, V4)

- **Trigger**: Hyperscaler announces CapEx increase or infrastructure buildout.
- **Method**: Trace the bill of materials downstream — from the platform to subsystems, components, substrates, and raw materials. Identify unknown or under-covered suppliers at each tier.
- **Edge case**: If the CapEx announcement lacks specificity (e.g., "we'll invest more in AI"), wait for procurement evidence (contracts, SEC filings, supply agreements) before building conviction.

### 2. Forward Revenue Disconnect (V2)

- **Trigger**: Market prices a stock on trailing P/E while forward revenue is 3-5x current.
- **Method**: Build a bottoms-up forward revenue model from contracts, backlog, and capacity. Compare the implied forward P/E to the trailing P/E the market is using.
- **Edge case**: If forward revenue depends on a single mega-contract not yet signed, discount it by probability of close and treat as catalyst-contingent.

### 3. Bottleneck Discovery (V3)

- **Trigger**: Physical supply chain mapping reveals a sole-source or highly concentrated supplier.
- **Method**: Confirm the bottleneck using three criteria: demand/supply ratio, market share concentration, and substitution timeline. Assess pricing power and margin trajectory.
- **Edge case**: A company can be a bottleneck without being a good investment — if margins are capped by long-term contracts or the bottleneck is transitional, pricing power doesn't translate to equity upside.

### 4. IV Mispricing (V1, V2)

- **Trigger**: Implied volatility is compressed (low percentile) on a stock where you have a high-conviction fundamental thesis.
- **Method**: LEAPS become cheap leverage when IV is low and the fundamental thesis has a 3-12 month catalyst. Compare the cost of LEAPS exposure to equivalent share position sizing.
- **Edge case**: Low IV can reflect genuine low expected movement. Confirm that IV compression is mispricing, not correct pricing, by checking whether the catalyst timeline is within the option's expiry.

### 5. SI Trap (V1)

- **Trigger**: Short interest exceeds 20% on a company with strong or improving fundamentals.
- **Method**: Validate that the short thesis is outdated or wrong. If fundamentals are strengthening while shorts are crowded, the asymmetry favors longs — any positive catalyst forces covering.
- **Edge case**: High SI on a fundamentally deteriorating company is justified, not a trap. The rule only applies when fundamentals contradict the short thesis.

### 6. Catalyst-First (V2)

- **Trigger**: A known catalyst with a definable timeline — S&P 500 inclusion, mega-contract announcement, policy action, earnings inflection.
- **Method**: Position ahead of the catalyst. Size based on catalyst probability and magnitude.
- **Edge case**: This is the one pattern where entering before a fear-driven dip is acceptable. The catalyst timeline overrides the "enter on drops" preference.

### 7. Sector Rotation Arbitrage (V4)

- **Trigger**: A sector moves broadly but one stock lags for non-fundamental reasons (liquidity, coverage gap, index exclusion).
- **Method**: Confirm the laggard's fundamentals are comparable or superior to peers that have moved. The lag itself is the entry signal.
- **Edge case**: If the lag is caused by a company-specific fundamental problem (customer loss, margin compression, management change), it is not rotation arbitrage — it is correct repricing.

### 8. Geopolitical/Policy Discovery (V4)

- **Trigger**: Government policy action creates a structural market shift — export controls, executive orders, national security designations, wartime mobilization, reshoring mandates.
- **Method**: Map the beneficiaries and casualties of the policy change. Identify which companies gain structural moats (e.g., domestic production becoming mandatory) and which lose competitive position. Assess whether the shift is permanent or temporary.
- **Edge case**: Political rhetoric without legislative backing is noise. Confirm the policy is enacted and enforceable before building conviction. "Never bet against enacted policy" — but do discount announced-but-unimplemented policy.

### 9. Sector Thesis Formation (V4, V6)

- **Trigger**: Multiple individual theses (Pattern 1-8) fire within the same sector or supply chain tier, revealing a structural driver common to all.
- **Method**: (1) Recognize pattern across individual picks. (2) Identify the structural driver (e.g., hyperscaler CapEx funneling into a tier). (3) Build internal hierarchy within the sector based on integration depth, margin structure, balance sheet quality, and contract visibility. (4) Consolidate capital into the highest-ranked name as conviction matures.
- **Edge case**: A sector thesis requires the structural driver to be durable. If the common driver is a single contract or temporary demand spike, treat each company individually rather than as a sector play.

### 10. Event Propagation Analog (V4)

- **Trigger**: A company-specific catalyst (S&P inclusion, mega-contract, business pivot) occurs, and a structurally similar company exists that has not yet received the same catalyst.
- **Method**: Identify structural similarity (revenue model, customer base, growth trajectory). Assess probability that the same event will propagate. Position in the analog company ahead of the expected event.
- **Edge case**: Structural similarity is necessary but not sufficient — the analog company must independently qualify on fundamentals. A weaker company does not automatically receive the same catalyst just because it is in the same sector.

## Seven-Stage Thesis Lifecycle

### 1. Discovery

Pattern recognition triggers initial interest. One of the ten formation patterns fires.
- **Judgment**: Is this pattern genuine or superficial? Does it warrant time investment?
- **Values**: V3 (supply chain mapping), V4 (cross-domain synthesis)

### 2. Evidence Gathering

SEC filings, supply chain mapping, financial statement analysis, institutional flow tracking, and management commentary.
- **Judgment**: Is the evidence primary (filings, contracts) or secondary (analyst estimates, media)? Primary evidence takes precedence.
- **Values**: V2 (numbers first), V8 (independent analysis)

### 3. Conviction Building

Forward P/E gate passes. Fundamental health gates clear. Bottleneck confirmed if applicable.
- **Judgment**: Does the evidence converge? If health gates fail but the thesis is otherwise strong, is the failure transient or structural?
- **Values**: V2 (quantitative primacy), V7 (intellectual honesty — don't force conviction)

### 4. Sizing

Conviction determines size. This is the capital commitment signal.

| Conviction Level | Position Size | Condition |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Starter | 1-2% | Initial thesis, incomplete evidence |
| Conviction | 3-5% | Thesis validated, multiple confirmations |
| High Conviction | 5-7% | All gates passed, catalyst timeline clear |

- **Values**: V5 (conviction through capital), V6 (concentrated bets)

### 5. PT Adjustment

Real-time evidence (earnings, contract wins, guidance changes) adjusts the price target up or down. This is math recalibration, NOT thesis mutation. The thesis itself remains intact unless a kill signal fires.
- **Judgment**: Distinguish between "new data changes the fair value estimate" and "new data changes the thesis." The former adjusts PT; the latter triggers Stage 7.
- **Values**: V2 (quantitative recalibration)

### 6. Monitoring

Track kill signals, earnings calendar, catalyst calendar, and sector health.
- **Judgment**: Is complacency setting in? Periodically reconstruct the bear case (V7). If you can't articulate why you're still in the position, reduce size.
- **Values**: V7 (intellectual honesty), V2 (ongoing fundamental tracking)

### 7. Exit

Exit when: a kill signal fires, the thesis is fully priced in, or a higher-conviction opportunity requires capital reallocation.
- **Judgment**: "Fully priced in" means current price exceeds growth-case valuation. Not "it went up a lot."
- **Values**: V6 (capital reallocation to highest conviction)

## Ten Tacit Decision Rules

### 1. Forward P/E Gate (V2)

- **Rule**: Never enter if forward P/E exceeds sector comparable at declining growth.
- **Application**: Screen every entry candidate against sector median forward P/E. Declining growth + premium valuation = avoid regardless of narrative.
- **Exception**: Hyper-growth company with no true sector comparable (create a custom peer set from adjacent industries).

### 2. Enter Only on Drops (V1)

- **Rule**: Preferred entry is fear-driven dips with fundamentals intact, not breakouts.
- **Application**: Wait for sentiment-driven selloffs (broad market, sector rotation, earnings overreaction) to enter fundamentally strong names.
- **Exception**: Catalyst-First pattern (Pattern 6) may require pre-catalyst entry when the timeline is fixed.
- **Prerequisite (V2)**: The Forward P/E Gate (Rule 1) must clear BEFORE this rule applies. A dip on a fundamentally overvalued or narrative-driven stock is correct repricing, not a buying opportunity. Dip-buying applies only when fundamental reality is established.

### 3. 30% Entry Protocol (V6)

- **Rule**: 30% of target position at initial conviction, 30% on confirmation, 40% on thesis strengthening.
- **Application**: Scales into positions as evidence accumulates. Prevents full-size entry on incomplete information.
- **Exception**: Day/swing trades on fear spikes may warrant full entry immediately due to time sensitivity.

### 4. Sector Catch-Up (V4)

- **Rule**: When a sector moves but one stock lags for non-fundamental reasons, buy the laggard.
- **Application**: Confirm the lag is not company-specific deterioration. The sector tide is the thesis; the lag is the entry.
- **Exception**: If the laggard has materially worse fundamentals than moved peers, the lag may be warranted.

### 5. SI > 20% = Buy Signal (V1, V8)

- **Rule**: If fundamentals are strong and short interest exceeds 20%, the short thesis is likely wrong.
- **Application**: Validate that the short thesis is based on outdated or incorrect information. Crowded shorts + improving fundamentals = asymmetric long.
- **Exception**: If the short thesis is based on fraud or accounting concerns you cannot disprove, respect the shorts.

### 6. IV-Driven Instrument Selection (V2)

- **Rule**: IV percentile determines the expression vehicle.
- **Application**: Low IV → LEAPS (cheap leverage). High IV → cash-secured puts (harvest premium). Mid-range → shares.
- **Exception**: For generational-conviction positions, shares may be preferred regardless of IV for simplicity and no expiry risk.

### 7. Seasonal Rotation (V1)

- **Rule**: Build positions in tax-harvested names during November-December for the January effect.
- **Application**: Identify fundamentally strong names that have been sold for tax-loss harvesting. The selling is mechanical, not fundamental.
- **Exception**: If macro regime is in severe risk-off, seasonal effects are overwhelmed. Macro overrides seasonal.

### 8. Follow Institutional Flow (V8)

- **Rule**: Track institutional ownership changes via 13F filings, IO% trends, and fund-type quality as thesis confirmation signals.
- **Application**: Classify institutional holders by type — passive accumulation (Vanguard/BlackRock index inclusion) is the strongest positive signal indicating structural, long-term demand. High quant/market-maker concentration indicates unstable, short-term positioning and may be a negative signal. IO% increase during fear-driven selloffs is strong confirmation of the thesis.
- **Exception**: Institutional flow during index rebalancing periods is mechanical, not signal. Confirm the flow is discretionary, not forced.

### 9. Necessary vs Discretionary (V3)

- **Rule**: Prefer companies providing necessary products over discretionary ones.
- **Application**: In uncertain macro, necessary-product companies have more resilient revenue. Supply chain bottlenecks in necessary products have stronger pricing power.
- **Exception**: In confirmed risk-on regimes with strong consumer spending, discretionary leaders can outperform.

### 10. Power-Law Allocation = Conviction (V5, V6)

- **Rule**: Portfolio follows a power-law distribution — 3-5 core positions hold 60-80% of capital reflecting highest conviction, while 15-25 satellite positions at 0.2-2% each provide optionality, sector coverage, and swing trade vehicles. Position size IS the conviction signal.
- **Application**: If adding a core position requires diluting an existing high-conviction core position, the new position must clear a higher bar. Satellite positions can be added freely without this trade-off. The top 3-5 names carry the portfolio's alpha; the tail is for optionality and learning.
- **Exception**: During regime transitions or thesis discovery phases, the core may temporarily thin while satellites grow as new theses are evaluated.

## Signal vs Noise Filter

**Universal filter** (V2): "Does this change forward revenue?"

- If yes → material. Investigate further — quantify the revenue impact, trace supply chain implications, assess whether the market has priced it in.
- If no → noise. Ignore regardless of headline intensity, social media volume, or short-term price action.

**Bear case construction** (V7): Every conviction long MUST have an explicit bear case. If you cannot articulate the bear case, you do not understand the position well enough to hold it. The bear case is not a formality — it is the kill signal watchlist.

## Eight Kill Signals

Thesis invalidation triggers. Each requires a specific response.

### 1. MC/Valuation Disconnect (V2)

Market cap loses all fundamental anchoring — price is entirely narrative-driven with no revenue or earnings path to justify it.
- **Response**: Exit. No partial trim — if the fundamental anchor is gone, the position has no thesis.

### 2. Suspicious Fundamentals (V7)

Restatements, auditor changes, unexplained accounting shifts, or revenue recognition anomalies.
- **Response**: Exit immediately. Do not wait for clarification. The asymmetry flips — downside from fraud far exceeds upside from innocence.

### 3. Meme Trap (V2)

Stock becomes meme-driven with price disconnected from any fundamental thesis.
- **Response**: Trim to zero. A meme stock can go higher, but the risk/reward is no longer analyzable.

### 4. Lockup Expiration (V2)

IPO lockup expiring with insider incentive to sell (underwater options, liquidation preference thresholds).
- **Response**: Reduce or hedge. Supply overhang is quantifiable — model the potential selling volume.

### 5. Inverse Cathie Wood (V8)

ARKK accumulation as a contrarian warning that a stock may be entering the hype cycle peak.
- **Response**: Increase monitoring frequency. Not an automatic exit, but a signal to tighten thesis discipline and re-examine the bear case.

### 6. Sector Collapse Signal (V3)

Industry-specific leading indicator crashes — NAND/DRAM spot prices for memory, wafer fab utilization for semis, container rates for logistics.
- **Response**: Reassess the entire chain. A sector-level collapse signal affects all positions in the chain, not just the direct company.

### 7. CapEx Cancellation (V3)

Downstream customer cancels or delays CapEx that was a key revenue assumption.
- **Response**: Immediate thesis review. Quantify the revenue impact. If the cancelled CapEx was more than 20% of your forward revenue model, the thesis may be broken.

### 8. Serial Dilution History (V2, V7)

Management team has a track record at prior companies of destroying shareholder value through repeated ATM dilutions. When the same management appears at a new company, any dilution announcement is treated as a kill signal because the behavioral pattern is established.
- **Response**: Exit on first dilution announcement. The behavioral pattern — serial dilution at previous companies — means the current company's management has demonstrated they will prioritize capital raises over shareholder value. Do not wait for "this time is different."
- **Why this matters (V2)**: Fundamental reality requires honest management. A company with serial-diluting management cannot be trusted for long-term thesis construction, regardless of how attractive the fundamentals appear.

## Dynamic Conviction Management (V9)

**Principle**: Conviction is a continuous variable, not a binary state. Static conviction is the enemy of capital efficiency. As markets are dynamic, conviction must be dynamic — strengthening, weakening, transferring, and converting to learning.

### Conviction Strengthening

When a high-conviction thesis experiences a significant drawdown WITHOUT any kill signal firing, asymmetry increases (V1). The correct response is to verify all kill signals are clear, then increase position size — not merely maintain it. The market is offering a better price on the same thesis.

**Judgment framework**: (1) Re-check every kill signal. (2) If all clear, evaluate whether the drawdown cause is mechanical (V10) or fundamental. (3) If mechanical or sentiment-driven, the lower price creates better risk/reward. (4) Scale up by one conviction tier.

**CAVEAT**: This is NOT "averaging down blindly." The prerequisite is a thorough kill signal review. If even one kill signal is ambiguous, do not escalate.

### Conviction Erosion

When a thesis fails to catalyze within its expected timeframe — no kill signal fires, but no positive catalyst materializes either — conviction erodes gradually. The methodology must accommodate this intermediate state between "monitoring" and "exit."

**Judgment framework**: (1) Compare elapsed time to the original catalyst timeline. (2) If 2x the expected timeframe has passed without catalyst, trigger formal thesis re-examination. (3) If the thesis still holds but urgency has faded, reduce position size to satellite level. (4) If the thesis cannot be re-articulated with fresh conviction, exit.

**Why this matters (V7)**: Pretending conviction is unchanged when it has eroded is intellectual dishonesty. Zombie positions — held out of inertia rather than conviction — dilute portfolio alpha.

### Thesis Inheritance

When a thesis succeeds on one company, the transferable elements (formation pattern, sector dynamics, customer profile, margin structure) can be systematically applied to similar candidates.

**Judgment framework**: (1) Extract the pattern elements that drove the success. (2) Identify new candidates that match these elements. (3) Validate independently — inheritance provides a hypothesis, not a conclusion. (4) The new candidate must pass all standard gates (V2, health gates) on its own merit.

**CAVEAT**: Thesis inheritance accelerates discovery but must not bypass validation. The template is a starting point for analysis, not a shortcut around it.

### Post-Mortem Protocol (V7)

After exiting a losing position, classify the loss to enable learning:

- **Category A — Correct thesis, wrong timing**: Thesis ultimately validated but position was exited or expired before catalyst. Learning: adjust time horizon assumptions.
- **Category B — Partially wrong thesis**: Some thesis elements were correct but material factors were missed. Learning: identify the blind spot.
- **Category C — Completely wrong thesis**: Fundamental misread of the situation. Learning: what evidence was misinterpreted?
- **Category D — Process error**: Correct analysis but execution failure (wrong sizing, wrong instrument, ignored kill signal). Learning: fix the process.

### Tax Harvest Rotation (V1, V6)

During October-December, tax-loss harvesting creates mechanical selling pressure on losing positions. This selling is not fundamental — it creates buying opportunities for fundamentally strong names whose prices have been temporarily depressed by tax-driven flows. Plan re-entry in January when the mechanical pressure reverses.

## Price Mechanism Literacy (V10)

**Principle**: WHY a price moves matters as much as HOW MUCH it moves. Without understanding the mechanism behind a price change, every dip looks the same — and the "enter on drops" rule becomes dangerous.

### Mechanism Classification

When a stock drops significantly, classify the mechanism before responding:

- **Fundamental change**: New information that materially changes forward revenue. Response: re-evaluate thesis, possibly exit.
- **Market-maker hedging / option pinning**: Mechanical hedging around options expiry concentrations. Response: wait for expiry to pass, then enter if thesis intact.
- **Margin liquidation cascade**: Forced selling from leveraged positions being liquidated. Response: this is the best buying opportunity if fundamentals are unchanged — forced sellers are not expressing a view.
- **Dark pool institutional accumulation**: Price suppressed while large buyers accumulate through dark pools. Response: this is bullish if the institution is high-quality (V8).
- **Sector contagion**: Price drops because a peer in the same sector had bad news. Response: assess whether the contagion is justified (shared customer, shared supply chain) or unjustified (guilt by association).

### TA as Timing Tool

Technical analysis is explicitly NOT used for directional conviction — fundamentals, catalysts, and macro determine direction. However, support/resistance levels and price patterns are acceptable as TIMING tools on fundamentally validated names. They inform WHERE to enter, never WHETHER to enter.

### Short-Term Trading Framework

Day and swing trades use a different decision process than conviction positions. Entry is based on experience-informed pattern recognition (support levels, intraday timing, overnight gaps) combined with fundamental validation. These trades are sized as satellite positions (V6) and are never confused with conviction holds.

## Multi-Scale Synthesis (V4)

**Principle**: An event in one domain has effects that propagate across domains AND across scales. Alpha lives in the gaps — where 2nd and 3rd order effects have not yet been priced.

### Cross-Domain Chains

**Forward chain** (CapEx acceleration):
Hyperscaler CapEx increase → neocloud revenue growth → GPU demand surge → memory demand increase → substrate demand increase → each tier is a potential investment.

**Reverse chain** (CapEx cut):
Hyperscaler CapEx cut → neocloud revenue miss → GPU demand drop → memory demand drop → substrate demand drop → each tier is a potential risk.

**Application rule**: "One company's earnings are another company's demand signal." When a company reports, immediately trace the implications upstream and downstream. The reporting company's results are public; the supply chain implications are not yet priced.

**When chains conflict**: If one hyperscaler accelerates CapEx while another cuts, weight by market share and assess whether the net effect is positive or negative for the chain. Do not assume uniformity.

### Cross-Scale Synthesis

Theses form at multiple scales simultaneously:

- **Individual → Sector**: When multiple individual theses fire in the same sector (Pattern 9), they may reveal a structural driver common to all. The sector thesis is MORE than the sum of individual theses — it provides a framework for ranking and concentrating capital.
- **Macro → Individual**: Geopolitical events and policy changes (Pattern 8) create structural shifts that propagate downward to specific companies. The macro event identifies the OPPORTUNITY SPACE; supply chain analysis identifies the SPECIFIC BENEFICIARY.
- **Event → Analog**: When a company-specific catalyst occurs (Pattern 10), assess whether structurally similar companies will receive the same catalyst. This is predictive, not reactive.

### Inverse Proxy Validation

When a proxy company FAILS at what the target company does, this validates the target's moat depth. A competitor's failure to replicate is evidence of how hard the capability is to build. Ask: "Which well-funded entity tried this and failed? What does their failure tell us about the moat?"

## Four Time Horizon Buckets

| Bucket | Holding Period | Typical Pattern | Sizing | Example Trigger |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|--------|-----------------|
| Day/Swing | 1-5 days | Fear spike + fundamentals intact | Small (1-2%) | Broad market panic, sector overreaction |
| Catalyst | 1-3 months | Known event with timeline | Medium (2-4%) | S&P inclusion, earnings, contract award |
| Conviction | 3-12 months | Thesis validated, multiple confirmations | Large (5-7%) | Forward revenue disconnect, bottleneck confirmed |
| Generational | 1-3 years | Structural shift, "once-in-a-decade" | Maximum (7%+) | New technology cycle, geopolitical realignment |

**Bucket assignment rule**: The thesis determines the bucket, not the price action. A conviction-bucket position that drops 20% does not become a swing trade — it either remains conviction (if thesis intact) or exits (if kill signal fires).

**Bucket migration**: Positions can migrate upward (swing → catalyst → conviction) as evidence accumulates, but downward migration (conviction → swing) is a warning sign that conviction is eroding. If a position migrates down, re-examine whether it should be exited entirely.

## When Values Conflict

Values are ordered by priority when they conflict:

1. **V7 (Intellectual Honesty)** always wins — if the thesis is broken, exit regardless of other signals.
2. **V2 (Fundamental Reality)** overrides narrative — numbers beat story. Binary disqualifiers (zero revenue, serial diluter) override all other analysis.
3. **V9 (Dynamic Conviction)** governs position management — conviction must be actively managed, not assumed static.
4. **V1 (Asymmetric Risk/Reward)** governs entry timing — but V2 governs whether to enter at all.
5. **V3 (Supply Chain Graph)** and **V4 (Multi-Scale Synthesis)** are the analytical edge — they inform thesis formation, not thesis override.
6. **V10 (Price Mechanism)** governs response to price action — classify the mechanism before reacting.
7. **V5/V6 (Conviction/Power-Law)** govern sizing — but only after V2 and V7 have cleared the position.
8. **V8 (Institutional Flow)** is confirmatory, never primary — institutional flow supports a thesis but never creates one.
