# Risk Management & Position Execution

## WHY — Values & Principles

### Capital Preservation Supremacy (V1)

"How much can I lose?" comes before "How much can I gain?" — always, without exception. No analysis, no matter how compelling, justifies violating loss limits. Capital is the irreplaceable resource; opportunities are renewable. A stock can always be bought back. Lost capital requires exponentially higher returns to recover: a 50% loss demands a 100% gain just to break even.

This is not a suggestion — it is the foundational value from which all other trading rules derive. Minervini averaged 220% per year from 1994 to 2000 (a 33,500% compounded return) not because he found better stocks than everyone else, but because he PROTECTED his capital through eight bear markets while letting winners run during bull markets.

The turning point in his career was not learning a new technique — it was learning to EXECUTE: cutting losses without ego, refusing to average down, reducing size after wins and losses alike. The gap between knowing what to do and actually doing it is where all profits are made or destroyed.

### Asymmetric Architecture (V1 + V2)

The entire SEPA system is architecturally designed to produce asymmetric outcomes: many small losses and occasional huge gains. The VCP isn't just a pattern — it's a mechanism that enables tight stops. Pyramiding isn't just position building — it's ensuring that capital is allocated proportionally to conviction PROVEN BY PRICE ACTION, never by opinion.

At a 50% win rate, you can build a fortune. If your winners average 3-4x the size of your losers, 5 wins and 5 losses still produce substantial profit. Batting average matters far less than slugging percentage. The system is designed to be psychologically executable, not just theoretically sound.

## HOW — Interpretation Methodology

### Stop-Loss Discipline

The maximum loss per trade is 7-8% from purchase price — absolute maximum. The ideal is 5% or less. This is not a guideline; it is a non-negotiable rule.

The VCP structure enables tight stops naturally: the logical stop is just below the low of the last contraction. When VCP quality is "prime," the stop can be as tight as 3-5% from entry, creating an entry-level R:R of 3:1 or better before the trade even begins.

**Never lower a stop.** Once set, the stop only moves UP as the stock advances:
- **Breakeven stop**: Once the stock advances 2-3x the initial risk, move the stop to breakeven. From this point, it's a "free trade" — you either win or break even.
- **Trailing stop**: As the stock continues advancing, trail the stop using the 20-day MA or recent swing low — whichever is tighter.

### Re-entry After Stop-Out

Being stopped out does not invalidate the thesis — it invalidates the TIMING. If a stock triggers your stop-loss but the underlying SEPA setup remains intact (Stage 2, TT pass, earnings accelerating, VCP still forming), you can and should re-enter when a new proper entry point appears.

This is psychologically the hardest discipline because it requires admitting you were wrong on timing while maintaining conviction on the thesis. Amateurs avoid re-entry out of ego ("it already stopped me out, I won't touch it again"). Professionals evaluate objectively: is the setup still valid? If yes, the prior stop was noise, not signal.

Guidelines:
- Re-entry requires a NEW valid entry signal (new VCP pivot, new demand day) — not re-buying at the same price hoping it works this time
- Maximum 2-3 re-entries on the same stock. After 3 stops, the market is telling you something you're not seeing — step aside
- Each re-entry uses the SAME position sizing discipline as initial entry. Never increase size to "make back" what the stops cost

### Progressive Exposure (Pyramiding)

Do not commit full position size at once. Build position as the stock PROVES itself through price action:

1. **Initial entry**: At or near VCP pivot on volume surge. Start with base position size.
2. **First add**: After stock advances 2-3% above entry on continued volume. Add 50% of initial size.
3. **Second add**: After stock advances further and holds gains. Add 25% of initial size.

The key: **decreasing increments** (100 → 50 → 25). Never add more shares than your initial position. Never add at a price where the total position would show a loss. This ensures your average cost rises slowly while your exposure increases only as the stock confirms the thesis.

### Tennis Ball vs Egg — Pullback Assessment

After a breakout, how the stock pulls back reveals everything about institutional commitment:

- **Tennis Ball**: Pullback ≤ 7 days, < 7% depth, declining volume, quick recovery to new high. This is HEALTHY — institutions are buying dips, supply is being absorbed. HOLD and consider adding.
- **Egg**: Pullback ≥ 10 days OR ≥ 10% depth OR increasing volume during decline. This is WEAK — institutions may be distributing. Signal to REDUCE position. Dead drops don't bounce.

The distinction is not about the magnitude of the pullback alone — it's about the CHARACTER of the pullback. A 5% pullback over 3 days on declining volume (tennis ball) is constructive. A 5% pullback over 15 days on rising volume (egg) is destructive.

### Sell Rules

**Defensive sells (protecting capital):**
- Stock hits 7-8% stop-loss from entry → SELL immediately, no exceptions
- 3+ consecutive closes below 20-day MA → SELL (the trend that justified entry has broken)
- Stock breaks below 50-day MA on heavy volume → SELL

**Offensive sells (capturing profits):**
- **Climax top signs**: After an extended advance, watch for these THREE signals clustering together:
  - Largest single-day gain of the entire move
  - Exhaustion gap (gap up that fails to hold)
  - Volume surge without corresponding price progress (churning)
  When these appear together, take profits. This is NOT the time to add — it's the time to sell into strength.
- **P/E contraction begins**: After a period of P/E expansion, the first signs of P/E contraction (stock flat or declining while earnings still grow) signal the market is re-rating the stock downward. The growth deceleration thesis is forming.
- **Earnings deceleration**: When quarterly EPS growth rate starts declining (even if still positive), the acceleration phase is ending. This is often the beginning of the end for superperformance.

### Position Sizing

- **Maximum single position**: ~20-25% of portfolio. Concentrated enough to matter, diversified enough to survive a gap-down.
- **Normal position**: 10-15% of portfolio for standard SEPA setups.
- **Reduced position** (V1 override situations):
  - After a big win: Temporarily reduce to base size. Overconfidence after success is the most dangerous enemy.
  - After consecutive losses: Reduce size by 50%. Reassess before returning to normal.
  - Bear market environment: Cut all new position sizes by 50%+. Mostly cash.
  - Late-stage base (3rd+): Reduce by 30-50% due to higher failure probability.
  - Earnings report approaching: Consider whether gap risk justifies current size.

### Market Environment & Exposure

Market timing is NOT about predicting tops and bottoms — it's about adjusting exposure to the environment:

- **Bull market, early stage**: Full investment. Most superperformers emerge in the first 12-18 months of a new bull market. This is when leaders break out first, before the broad indexes confirm.
- **Bull market, late stage**: Leaders start topping while broad indexes march higher. Second-tier stocks take over "leadership" — a sign of deteriorating quality. Distribution day count rising (4+ in 25 days = warning). Begin tightening stops and reducing exposure.
- **Correction**: Cash is a position. Move to 30-50% cash. Only enter setups with exceptional SEPA convergence. Tighten all stops.
- **Bear market**: Mostly cash. 90%+ of superperformance occurs during bull markets. The odds of VCP breakout success collapse in bear environments. Preserve capital for the next bull.

Distribution day counting is the thermometer: a high-volume down day in the indexes = 1 distribution day. Track the count over 25 trading days. 4+ = the market is under distribution pressure, regardless of how the indexes "look" on the surface.

**Seasonal Pacing**: Treat the trading year like a 12-inning game. Don't jump in with full exposure after a correction or at the start of a new year. Start with pilot buys at 25-50% of normal position size to test whether the market will reward your type of setup. If pilot buys are not profitable, there is no reason to move to 100% exposure — the market is telling you its current theme doesn't match your methodology. Scale up only when early results confirm the environment is favorable.

### After-Run Psychology

Trading psychology is not separate from trading strategy — it IS the strategy:

- **After a big winner**: The most dangerous moment. Overconfidence inflates position sizes, loosens stop discipline, and leads to sloppy entries. Deliberately reduce to base position size. Wait for "normal mode" to return before scaling back up.
- **After consecutive losses**: Also dangerous, but for different reasons. The temptation is either to "make it back" with a bigger bet (revenge trading) or to give up entirely. Neither is correct. Reduce size by 50%, verify your process is still sound, and trade small until confidence returns naturally through results.
- **The ego trap**: Trading is about making money, not about being right. Separating your ego from your trades is the single most transformative psychological shift. When you stop needing to be right and start needing to make money, you will cut losses without hesitation and let winners run without fidgeting.

### Style Commitment

Commit to one approach and master it. You cannot be the best value trader AND the best growth trader AND the best day trader AND the best long-term investor. Like a doctor who cannot be the best brain surgeon, heart surgeon, AND psychiatrist simultaneously — specialization is required.

There will be periods when your style underperforms. Accept this. Do not switch styles mid-cycle to chase what's currently working. The master who has practiced one kick 10,000 times is more dangerous than the one who has practiced 10,000 kicks once.

## WHEN — Score Caveats

### When Stop-Loss Rules Need Extra Consideration

- **Gap-down through stop**: If earnings or news causes a gap that blows through your stop level, sell at the open. The loss will be larger than planned, but holding and hoping for recovery is the greater danger. Accept the outsized loss, reduce subsequent position sizes, and move on.
- **Earnings season proximity**: If a stock is approaching its earnings report within 1-2 weeks and you're sitting on a small gain or loss, consider the gap risk. A 5% position with 15% gap risk = 0.75% portfolio impact. An 20% position with 15% gap risk = 3% portfolio impact. Size accordingly.
- **50% win rate is sufficient**: If this feels uncomfortable, remember: 10 trades at 50% win rate with average winner = 3x average loser produces a 100% gain on capital risked. The math works because asymmetric R:R (enabled by VCP + tight stops) means individual trade outcomes don't need to be right more often than wrong.
- **Never average down**: This is absolute. Adding to a losing position is committing MORE capital to a trade that is already proving you wrong. The stock can always be re-purchased later at a proper entry point. What cannot be recovered is the capital lost from averaging into a declining position.
