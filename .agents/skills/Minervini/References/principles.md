# Principles — the full in-scope set, by funnel stage

Depth layer for the backbone. Each principle: **claim** → *Why (mechanism)* → *Instance*. Where a principle reverses a sensible-seeming default, the default it overrides is named. The backbone already argues the load-bearing ~8 in full; those appear here only briefly, with their instance, for completeness — the words are spent on what the backbone does **not** cover.

**On the *Instance* — and on every number below.** An Instance is a **precedent**, not a prescription: a named case that actually happened (Dell EPS 80→65→28% then −80%), there to make the mechanism concrete and stick. Its figures are non-replicable *outcomes* — treat them as a story that generalizes, never as a gate to copy onto another stock. That is *why* the instances earn their place rather than anchoring you: you cannot paste "+360%" or "−99%" into a fresh case. The hazard is the other kind of number — a **band on a flexible window** (a contraction width, a base depth) that reads like a copyable cutoff. Where a real numeric trigger exists it lives in `spec.md` (terse, tunable, one place to change it), so any band appearing in a *claim* or *Why* here is **illustrating a relationship** — read it as "roughly," and reach for the relationship, not the digits.

Scope: this skill **discovers and analyzes**. Every risk-flavored item below is kept as a **diagnostic setup-quality read** — "this is what it MEANS" — never as a sizing, stop, or exposure prescription. Position math is out of scope and deliberately absent.

## 1. Regime — is the tide coming in at all?

**Over 90% of superperformers launch out of a correction or bear bottom.** *(backbone — brief)*
*Why:* a superperformer needs a rising tide of institutional risk-appetite to fund the supply/demand imbalance; regime is a first-order base rate, not an overlay, so grinding single-name screens deep in a bear is low-yield by construction.
*Instance:* ">90% began as the market came out of a correction/bear; very few during a bear."

**Time the market off LEADERSHIP and breadth — NOT a distribution-day count.** *(correction — this skill previously got this wrong)*
*Why:* leaders have low correlation to the averages and turn at extremes *before* the indexes do, so the index is a lagging timing tool. There is no follow-through-day and no distribution-day tally in the source method — that is a deliberate substitution, not an omission. Read the new-high/new-low spread and its expansion, the leadership list broadening or buckling, and your own stop-out rate. *Overrides default:* the CANSLIM/O'Neil lore that a momentum regime gate is built on a follow-through day plus a rolling distribution-day count. Do not attribute that mechanism here; distribution days are at most a secondary tape read, never the gate.
*Instance:* across the source, "follow-through" appears only in the single-stock sense (a stock following through above its pivot), never as a market-bottom confirmation day.

**New-high vs new-low EXPANSION is the breadth confirm; your own stop-out rate is a live regime gauge.**
*Why:* an expanding new-high list shows the rally broadening past the first leaders into real participation. And because sound criteria should work in a healthy tape, a *cluster of stop-outs after a winning record* is read as the market being wrong, not the picks — leaders break down before the index falls, so your fill quality is an early breadth sensor. The diagnosis is binary: repeated stop-outs mean either flawed criteria OR a hostile environment, nothing else. *Overrides default:* reading a losing streak as purely a stock-picking problem.
*Instance:* "confirmation is given when the list of stocks making new 52-week highs outpaces the new-low list and starts to expand significantly."

**The lockout rally: overbought-that-won't-pull-back is a BUY tell, not a wait signal.**
*Why:* off an important bottom the first leg refuses to give the awaited pullback (contained to ~3–5%). Persistent overbought that won't correct means demand is so deep no seller can move price down — the opposite of the mean-reversion the oscillator was built to flag. The confirming filter is breadth: your leader list expanding. *Overrides default:* "extremely overbought after a decline → wait for the pullback."
*Instance:* post-bear lockout, early-leg index pullbacks 3–5%; leaders pulse 15–20% up then rest only 5–10% (see Stage section).

**Group-breadth at the highs picks the bull's leadership — only ~3-4 to 8-10 groups matter.**
*Why:* leadership is narrow and identifiable by *breadth, not index level* — groups break to new highs BEFORE the benchmark bottoms, so the per-group 52-week-new-high count is a leading map of where the next several hundred percent concentrates. Concentrate in the best names of the top 4-5 sectors; spreading across laggard sectors dilutes into the average.
*Instance:* 1974 — mid/small-caps accumulated Sep–Oct though the indexes bottomed in December.

**The 'shot across the bow': rotation OUT of leaders INTO laggards/defensives marks the late stage.**
*Why:* the index can be held aloft by rotation while leadership has already broken, so the index LEVEL is a lagging, misleading top-indicator — *which* stocks carry it is the leading internal. Money moving into drugs, tobacco, utilities, food, even as the indexes track higher on the stragglers, is an early warning weeks-to-months ahead of the top. "Keep your sights on the trees, not the forest."
*Instance:* "cash rotates into laggard stocks… the indexes track higher on the backs of the stragglers… the end is near."

**Low-valuation factors are the WORST place to hide in a crash.**
*Why:* a low multiple is itself a symptom of fundamentals already being marked down; when forward expectations collapse there is no cushion under names the market had already discounted, so the "margin of safety" evaporates exactly when needed. *Overrides default:* "value/cheap = defensive in a downturn."
*Instance:* 2008 — low P/S −66.9%, low P/B −68.8%, low P/E −70.9% vs Dow −34%.

**Most funds cannot raise more than 5–10% cash even in dreadful conditions — so they MUST lose in bear markets.**
*Why:* benchmark-relative measurement plus career risk is a hard mandate to stay ~90%+ invested regardless of regime; the loss is designed-in, not a skill failure. The individual's freedom to hold 100% cash is therefore quantifiable structural alpha — capital preservation across regimes is half the track record, equal to selection, because a single un-exited bear erases years of compounding. *Overrides default:* "the pros must be managing the downside."
*Instance:* managers boast of being down "only 32%" when the market is −40%.

**Superperformance is systematically misread as 'risky.'** *(reframe under strength-begets-strength)*
*Why:* risk perception is anchored to consensus comfort, not to actual loss distributions (Keynes: the banker prefers to be ruined conventionally), so deviation is deterred by social/career risk, not financial risk — which is exactly why the edge persists. The tell that you may be onto real edge: competent peers call it risky from unfamiliarity, not evidence.

## 2. Trend / Stage — the eligibility gate, and where in the sequence you stand

**The Trend Template is a hard gate, not a score — all eight, technicals BEFORE fundamentals.** *(backbone — brief; full numbers in spec.md)*
*Why:* technicals-first is a forcing function against the analyst's strongest bias (loving a story while the tape says institutions aren't buying); all-eight-or-reject removes the discretionary fudge where one weak leg is rationalized by a strong one. No fundamental quality buys an exception.

**The empirical base rate: 99% / 96% above the 200d / 50d MA before the move.**
*Why:* converts "trend matters" into a falsifiable prior — buying a below-200d name bets on a <1% tail. It quantifies exactly how much edge you forfeit by being early and justifies treating MA position as near-absolute, not a soft preference.

**Set the trend-stage gate BEFORE reading any base — context vetoes the pattern.**
*Why:* an ORDERING rule, not merely "trend is your friend." Gating on Stage 2 first means you never get seduced by a beautiful base whose context makes it failure-prone (too-early → the downtrend resumes; obvious late-stage base → crowded). The amateur habit is to look at the consolidation first. *Overrides default:* evaluating a base "on its own merits."
*Instance:* "going long a great base in a long-term downtrend is like saying you're healthy because of low cholesterol when you have pneumonia."

**Stage-2 confirmation requires a PRIOR 25–30% rally off the 52-week low — sometimes far more.**
*Why:* the required prior advance is the COST of confirmation — you surrender the first leg in exchange for evidence institutional money is actually flowing. Buying lower means buying in Stage 1/4, where by definition there is no momentum to compound. *Overrides default:* "wait for confirmation" is known; the 25–30% minimum (often 80%) magnitude is the content.
*Instance:* Amgen — +80% before he'd even consider it.

**~95% of Trend-Template survivors die at the fundamental/RS/volatility screen.**
*Why:* a clean uptrend is the easy, abundant qualifier; the real scarcity is fundamentals × relative strength. This calibrates where the funnel actually narrows — don't spend judgment budget admiring charts; spend it confirming the rarer layer 95% lack.

**Higher-lows-into-the-final-leg, and stair-step base-building, is the first-wave leadership signature.**
*Why:* when indexes make LOWER lows in the last leg of a decline, true leaders make HIGHER lows — and a stock can improve its RS rank merely by marking time sideways. The RS line is a real-time readout of which names institutions refuse to sell under maximum index pressure; the moment a name stops confirming new lows, supply on it specifically is exhausted, which precedes both the stock's and the market's turn. Stair-stepping (supply absorbed at progressively higher floors) is sturdier than a V/spike-bottom — so "strength" can look like dead-flat price. *Overrides default:* waiting for the general market to confirm, and missing the leaders that bottom first.
*Instance:* Amgen (1990) "moved sideways and marked time… improved its RS rank without rallying much," then +360%; Lumber Liquidators made a higher low while the Nasdaq made a lower low, then ran 90%.

**Emerging-leader pulse off a bottom: advance 15–20%, then rest with only a 5–10% pullback.** *(climax/bottom diagnostic — gap re-mine)*
*Why:* the shallow-pullback rhythm is the price signature of genuine accumulation (demand absorbs supply on dips). It is a diagnostic of base quality across the whole leadership *cohort*, not a single name — if most broken-out leaders instead crash and can't rally, the bottoming read is invalidated and it is too early. *Overrides default:* "a leader needs deep pullbacks to be 'healthy.'"

**Climax run / blow-off: parabolic acceleration ENDS the trend, it doesn't extend it.** *(climax-top diagnostic — gap re-mine)*
*Why:* a stock climbing in orderly stair-step bases that suddenly turns near-vertical is taking out the final heavy institutional demand — velocity that steep discounts everything at once and leaves no marginal buyer behind it, so it is unsustainable by construction. Here acceleration is an *exhaustion* read, not strength to chase. *Overrides default:* "accelerating price = strengthening momentum = add; steeper is better." (Contrast: earnings acceleration is bullish; *price* climax acceleration is the top.)
*Instance:* Stryker 1991 topped after +65% in 11 weeks, rolled into a Stage-3 top then Stage-4 decline; the next base took five years.

**200-day flattens, rolls over, and price whipsaws ACROSS it = Stage-3 topping diagnostic.** *(climax-top diagnostic — gap re-mine)*
*Why:* the 200-day is the institutional trend proxy; when net demand stops lifting it the average loses slope, and price's repeated round-trips across it are the visible tug-of-war as distribution overwhelms remaining accumulation. The whipsaw-across-a-flattening-average is the signature — distinct from, and earlier than, the simple "below the 200-day" rule. Stage 4 is confirmed only once the average is in a definite downtrend with price living below it. *Overrides default:* "fine until decisively below the 200-day."

**Deceleration from a high base tops the stock — judged against its OWN history.**
*Why:* during the momentum phase price is priced for the *prior* rate; the first material deceleration breaks the discounting regardless of how good the absolute level still looks, because the market trades the delta from expectation, not the level. *Overrides default:* "still growing 28% is fine."
*Instance:* Dell EPS growth 80→65→28% (1995–99) topped despite "decent growth," then −80%; Home Depot 41→11%.

**Broken Leader Syndrome — the former superperformer at −70% is a Stage-4 short, not a re-entry.**
*Why:* a leader topping is the highest-information fundamental signal — the aggregate judgment that growth is decelerating — and it *leads* the reported numbers, so trailing-earnings valuation reads "cheap" exactly when forward reality is worst. GARP/PEG/low-P/E screens that flag it are precisely the bait. (This is the diagnostic frame for *why* you avoid Stage 4: company survival ≠ capital survival — see Risk-as-setup-quality.) *Overrides default:* "buy the quality name that's come down."
*Instance:* Sun Microsystems topped late 2000; buyers at −75% lost a further ~80%, ultimately −99%.

**The 'growth stock' label is itself a sell tell — the earnings-maturation cycle.**
*Why:* Value → positive surprises/upward revisions/EPS-momentum → officially "growth stock" (everybody knows) → loss of momentum → negative surprise back to Value. The label is a coincident-to-lagging indicator of *crowding*: by the time the story is obvious, early money is distributing to naive buyers chasing the press. Buy in the under-followed phase. *Overrides default:* the instinct to buy the celebrated, widely-recognized "growth stock."

**Cyclicals have an INVERTED P/E cycle.**
*Why:* for commodity/economically-sensitive cyclicals, P/E (trailing earnings vs a forward-discounting price) flips: at the trough, collapsed earnings inflate the multiple while price already anticipates recovery; at the peak, record earnings crush the multiple while price anticipates the downturn. So a very HIGH P/E marks the buy zone and a very LOW P/E the sell zone — applying a growth-stock earnings lens mis-times it entirely. *Overrides default:* "low P/E = cheap, buy."
*Instance:* Lynch — buying a cyclical at a low P/E after record earnings is "a proven method for losing half your money."

**Industry maturity reads off the firm-count curve, not the growth rate.**
*Why:* every innovation traces a penetration-to-saturation S-curve where the *number* of firms rises to a peak then collapses; that firm-count peak (then margin collapse, bankruptcies) marks the growth→replacement transition. Competitor-count is a cleaner phase marker than sales: rising = land-grab; rolling over = shakeout.
*Instance:* autos peaked ~77 firms (1920)→~15 (1960); disk drives 75 (1984)→20 (1998); PCs 100 (1987)→10 (1992).

## 3. VCP / Setup — the geometry of the base and the precise trigger

**VCP: each contraction halves the prior pullback.** *(spec.md has the notation)*
*Why:* each pullback's depth is a live readout of remaining sellable supply — as institutions absorb float, fewer sellers rush each rally, so swings mechanically shrink. The content is the **ratio and the count**, not any specific width: each contraction roughly *halves* the prior (so a base might run ~25→12→6%, but the start could as well be 40% or 8%), over 2–4 — occasionally 5–6 — contractions. That progression is the test that absorption is genuinely advancing, not just chopping. *Overrides default:* "tightening base" is known; the halving and the count are the content.
*Instance:* VIVO 40W 31/3 4T (31→17→8→3%), +118%.

**Pivot volume must dry to BELOW the 50-day average — to near the base's lowest.**
*Why:* volume IS supply made visible — a volume vacuum at the pivot proves incremental sellers have stopped coming, so even small demand snaps price up with no resistance. The counterintuitive part: the very illiquidity that scares most investors is the precise tell of readiness. *Overrides default:* reading a liquidity dry-up before a move as bearish.
*Instance:* AMSC late-Dec 2003 contracted to near-zero before +60%; Best Buy 1997's lowest-volume day was the day BEFORE breakout.

**Volume asymmetry: up-days must dwarf down-days, by several hundred to ~1,000%.**
*Why:* institutional accumulation can't hide its footprint — buying in size shows as outsized up-volume with a conspicuous dearth of down-spikes; the *asymmetry* (not the level) distinguishes sponsorship from a crowd bounce. A big demand day FOLLOWED by an even bigger down-volume day disqualifies. *Overrides default:* "volume confirms breakouts" is known; the up-vs-down asymmetry test and the disqualifier are the content.

**The footprint notation reduces a base to three scalars: time / depth-pair / T-count.** *(reorganizing frame)*
*Why:* "40W 31/3 4T" first-classes the variables that carry supply/demand info — how long supply digested, how deep the worst flush vs how dry it is now, how many absorption cycles — and discards visual noise, making right-edge tightness directly comparable to the worst flush. *Overrides default:* holistic "pattern recognition" by overall shape.

**On a tight pivot, early entry buys risk, not edge.**
*Why:* the pivot's whole function is the final filter — the point where price confirms demand has won; front-running it saves negligible price but takes the full risk of an unconfirmed setup, so it is strictly negative-EV. Let price trade above the pivot first. *Overrides default:* the instinct to anticipate the breakout for a better basis.

**Time compression: a too-fast right side (V-shape) disqualifies the setup.**
*Why:* supply takes TIME to digest — a quick up-down recovery with no proper right-side development hasn't relieved weak holders, so the overhang is still live even though price looks recovered. The required left/right symmetry is the tell that digestion was real, not a reflexive bounce. *Overrides default:* buying a sharp V-recovery to the highs as bullish strength.
*Instance:* MGA Nov 2010 "ran up the right side too quickly," needed more base, then +140%.

**The explosive-surge-then-tight-recovery pre-breakout signature.**
*Why:* a violent demand surge (e.g. +100% in a week, or a single-day catalyst gap of 200–270%) on huge volume measures the size of anticipating demand; the *shallowness and speed* of the subsequent recovery measures whether that demand persists vs a one-off spike. The recovery quality is the load-bearing filter, not the surge. *Overrides default:* treating a huge spike as already over-extended, or chasing the spike directly.
*Instance:* HGSI +270% in one day on drug data, then +165% over 7 months.

**The 3C / cup-completion-cheat is the quantified earliest legitimate cup buy.** *(spec.md has the qualifiers)*
*Why:* it lets you buy the turn of an intermediate correction inside an intact Stage 2 at a lower basis. The >60% correction ceiling encodes a supply truth (too much overhead for clean continuation); the 5–10% cheat plateau is the same range-exhaustion signal as a handle. Never earlier than the cheat.
*Instance:* Google 2004 rare "low cheat," no handle → +600% in 38 weeks.

**Primary-base depth tolerance scales with base duration.** *(spec.md has the tiers)*
*Why:* time absorbs supply — a deep BUT long correction (up to 50% over ~a year) has washed out weak holders and rebuilt support; a deep AND *fast* correction signals genuine demand failure because price collapsed before constructive absorption could occur. *Overrides default:* "bases shouldn't be too deep" is known; the duration-keyed tiers are the content.

**Flat-base buy point and the 10–15% correction ceiling.**
*Why:* the shallowness ceiling is a supply test — a flat base that held within 10–15% proves sellers never gained control, so the high is a clean line of least resistance; you buy above the base HIGH. Knowing which patterns pivot at the high (flat base) vs below it (cup-with-handle, 3C) tells you WHERE to set each trigger.

**Base count 3–5 marks the late stage; bases 1–2 off a correction are the prime entry.**
*Why:* base count proxies how "discovered" and crowded the institutional accumulation has become — each successive base taps more of the remaining heavy demand, so late bases coincide with accumulation getting too obvious, the moment distribution starts to overwhelm. Base 3 is more obvious but tradable; by 4–5 the trend is glaringly obvious and abrupt failures multiply. *Overrides default:* reading any clean base-breakout as equally good regardless of WHERE in the Stage-2 sequence it sits.

**The Primary Base is the first buyable base — never the IPO.**
*Why:* an IPO has no support structure and no proven holders — the float is dominated by flippers whose profit-taking guarantees an early correction; the primary base (first proper consolidation, emerging to an all-time high) is the market's first chance to absorb that supply and PROVE demand exceeds the overhang. The IPO pop and the post-IPO profit-taking selloff are explicitly NOT the buy point. *Overrides default:* "buy strength" on a hot IPO day one, or buying the post-IPO dip.
*Instance:* Amazon (public May 1997) broke out of its primary base ~4 months later, then +2,500% in 16 months.

**Engineer entry AFTER obvious-support shakeouts; buy the LAST weak hand out.** *(diagnostic / timing frame — sizing stripped)*
*Why:* whatever support is visible to you is visible to everyone and densely loaded with stops — "what is too obvious seldom works." As a stop-using buyer you are by definition a weak holder; the goal is to be the *last* one. Seeking bases that have undercut a prior obvious low one to three times means the trip-wire stops are already picked off and weak holders flushed, so little supply is left to stop you out, and the undercut-then-reclaim is positive confirmation the flush is complete. *Overrides default:* "get in early," and placing entry/stops "just below obvious support" — precisely the trap.
*Instance:* DKS Feb 2003 undercut its Dec 2002 low, then +525% over 54 months.

**Excessive post-IPO correction renders a stock unbuyable.** *(setup-quality diagnostic)*
*Why:* a new issue that craters too far too fast right after debut shows distribution overwhelmed demand at the most-hyped moment — the supply/demand verdict is already negative and there is no constructive base to break out from, so the entire entry logic has no anchor. "Cheap vs IPO price" is a trap on a technically broken stock. *Overrides default:* buying a hyped IPO after it's "come down to earth" 40–50%.
*Instance:* Facebook 2012 high $45 → −43% to $25.52 within 12 days, rendering it unbuyable.

## 4. Fundamentals × Quality — the rare layer ~95% of chart-passers lack

**Acceleration beats level — read the second derivative.** *(backbone — brief)*
*Why:* institutional valuation/surprise models re-rate on the *rate of change* of growth, not its level — an accelerating second derivative forces analysts to keep revising up and self-feeds the surprise machine. >90% of the biggest winners showed earnings acceleration before/during the move. *Overrides default:* wanting the highest absolute growth rate; you want growth getting *faster*.
*Instance:* −34→+12→+44→+83% beats any single high number.

**Code 33 — triple acceleration over exactly three quarters.** *(spec.md has the definition)*
*Why:* requiring EPS, revenue, AND net margin to all *accelerate* together is multiplicative — rising sales × rising margin compounds an earnings rate neither produces alone — and it automatically filters out cost-cut-driven single-lever "growth"; three quarters rules out a fluke while staying early. *Overrides default:* tracking earnings GROWTH and being satisfied by any high number.
*Instance:* Monster/Hansen 2003–05: EPS +75→214→195%, sales +20→63→93%, NPM 5.4→11.3→18.8%.

**Sales must corroborate EPS — triple-digit revenue is the leader tell.**
*Why:* revenue acceleration is the un-fakeable substrate — margin expansion and buybacks can manufacture EPS for a few quarters, but sustained superperformance requires real top-line demand; EPS without sales is "accounting gimmickry." The triple-digit-sales bar for emerging leaders is far higher than typical "good revenue" intuition.
*Instance:* Home Depot 1982–83 quarterly sales 104/158/191/220% before a 698% move.

**Fundamental quality is QUALITATIVE — there is no numeric ROE/debt/float gate.** *(correction — this skill previously got this wrong)*
*Why:* the source sets no return-on-equity floor, no leverage ceiling, no float cap. Quality is read structurally, and inventing numeric cutoffs the method doesn't contain manufactures false precision. The three structural reads that DO exist:
- **Pricing power + capital-intensity is a structural disqualifier.** A business with limited pricing power, capital-intensive operations, low/pressured margins, heavy regulation or intense competition caps the margin-expansion and price-raising levers that drive superperformance, so even strong reported earnings can't compound into a durable Stage-2 move. *Instance:* the airline industry — "doesn't have much pricing power, faces government regulatory pressures, is very capital intensive, and is highly commodity-sensitive because of fuel costs." The ideal inverse can raise prices, enter new markets, launch products AND cut costs.
- **Diagnose WHY a net margin is falling.** Cost-driven dips (raw materials, temporary inefficiency, taxes) are survivable and self-correcting; a decline because *selling prices* are dropping signals collapsing demand / lost pricing power and forecasts continued erosion — the terminal kind. *Instance:* "far more worrisome is when the net margin declines because prices are dropping as a result of declining customer interest."
- **Strip non-recurring GAINS before believing any growth number.** The market pays for sustainable operating earnings; a one-time gain (asset sale) inflates the headline without reflecting the ongoing business, and stripping it can flip the print from up to down. *Instance:* reported $3.01 vs $2.40 (+25%), but $0.84 was a non-strategic asset sale → adjusted $2.17 = DOWN ~7%.

**The lowered-bar beat is a red flag, not a green one.**
*Why:* a surprise only carries information relative to expectations the company *didn't* engineer — management can sandbag (warn → analysts cut → beat the cut) or take one big down quarter to reset low. The same headline "beat" inverts in meaning depending on who moved the bar. *Overrides default:* "any earnings beat = bullish," without conditioning on who lowered consensus.

**Guidance can move the stock more than the reported number.**
*Why:* price discounts the future — reported EPS is largely anticipated while guidance is the freshest delta; because companies are structurally conservative with guidance, a *voluntary raise* is a costly signal management won't issue unless it can beat even the raised bar. The cleanest setup is a beat PLUS raised guidance. *Overrides default:* anchoring the trade on the headline beat/miss.

**Read the P/E as an expectation barometer, not a valuation.**
*Why:* P/E measures what the market will pay for earnings — the *level of embedded expectation* — so the operative question isn't "cheap/expensive" but "can the company exceed the embedded expectation, and is that expectation still rising?" A high multiple is dangerous only if growth can't sustain it; a low one is opportunity only if expectations are about to inflect up. *Overrides default:* P/E as an objective valuation comparable across stocks.

**Inventory vs sales — read the finished-goods sub-segment, not the total.**
*Why:* the three inventory layers encode management's own private demand forecast — raw-materials buildup = a bet demand is RISING (bullish, should confirm in sales within a quarter or two); finished-goods buildup = already made more than it can sell (bearish). Inventory level alone is meaningless. *Overrides default:* treating all inventory buildup as equivalent.
*Instance:* red flag — total inventory +44% (raw +8%, WIP +24%, finished goods +79%), receivables +33%, sales +11%.

**Double-whammy — receivables AND inventory both outrunning sales by 2x+.** *(quality diagnostic, moved from risk cluster)*
*Why:* the two signals describe opposite ends of the same broken sales chain and corroborate — finished-goods buildup means more was made than the market absorbs; rising receivables means the company isn't even being paid for what it shipped. Together: end-demand failing AND customers can't pay — a high-probability earnings hit, not one yellow flag. *Overrides default:* receivables-vs-sales is known; the "≥2x on BOTH simultaneously" conjunction is the content.

**Repeated 'one-time' charges and book-vs-tax divergence are quality tells.**
*Why:* both are footprints of the same behavior — earnings are a managed narrative, and the lie shows where two reports of the same reality must reconcile. A "non-recurring" charge that recurs means the core number is permanently flattered; great earnings to shareholders + little tax paid (accrual books diverging from cash-basis IRS books) signals the reported number may be fiction. *Overrides default:* excluding every one-time charge on faith and never cross-referencing GAAP against tax footnotes.

**Contraction language is the institutional sell-tell of a spent grower.**
*Why:* expansion-phase firms describe their future in opening/hiring/new-market terms; the appearance of contraction vocabulary in guidance — close stores, open fewer, renegotiate leases, cut HQ staff — is a direct readout that the margin/scale flywheel reversed, a leading classifier independent of the current print. *Overrides default:* the value instinct that a restructuring/cost-cut plan is a bullish efficiency catalyst.
*Instance:* Circuit City 2008 planned to close 155 stores… then went to zero.

**Franchised earnings are lower-quality earnings — discount them.**
*Why:* franchise-fee income is one step removed from underlying unit economics and concentrates operational/failure risk onto operators the franchisor doesn't fully control, so the same headline EPS is more fragile than from owned stores. *Overrides default:* treating a dollar of franchise earnings as equal to a dollar of owned-store earnings.
*Instance:* 2007 McDonald's ~60% franchisee-operated.

**Demand proof of scalability for small caps; large-cap upside is structurally capped.**
*Why:* asymmetric due-diligence by supply/demand physics — a small float means demand moves price a lot (huge upside) but the business is unproven, so you substitute a scalability/already-profitable check for the missing record; a large float means proven execution but muted appreciation. *Overrides default:* one fundamental checklist regardless of size.

**Turnaround buy-gate: TTM-EPS recovering to the old peak + ~100% recent-quarter growth.** *(spec.md has the bar)*
*Why:* turnarounds face easy year-ago comps, so a triple-digit print is the minimum bar distinguishing a real inflection from a low-base optical bounce; anchoring to TTM-EPS recovering toward the prior peak filters cosmetic rebounds, and growth must NOT be cost-cut-only. *Overrides default:* the ~100% bar (far above the 20–25% floor) is the content.

**Fundamentally sound vs price-ready — sequence the two analyses.** *(good company ≠ good stock; backbone-adjacent)*
*Why:* meeting all fundamental criteria is NOT a buy signal — a company can report great quarters while the stock still corrects because price ran up in anticipation and now digests while earnings catch up. Price is set by the institutional supply/demand state, not fundamentals in isolation; until they establish a line of least resistance, the fuel exists but the ignition doesn't. *Overrides default:* "once fundamentals check out, buy promptly."

**Own the company's own earnings up-cycle, not the market's cycle.**
*Why:* price-relative-to-index proxies the company's INDEPENDENT earnings cycle — a stock that resists a bear decline is typically in its own up-cycle temporarily suppressed by the index; one that goes nowhere in a strong market is in its own *down*-cycle and should be avoided regardless of the tape. The crucial inverse: relative weakness in a strong tape = sell/avoid.
*Instance:* Panera +1,100% over 26 months while the Nasdaq fell ~80%.

**Superperformers' age window: public 8–10 years before the move; growth concentrated in the first 5–10 years.** *(spec.md has the window)*
*Why:* the window where management is "at its entrepreneurial best," products expand into untapped markets, and economies of scale compound margins is a finite, datable period opening at IPO and closing as the firm matures into an institutional favorite — so age post-IPO screens mechanically for the margin-acceleration curve.
*Instance:* 80% of 1990s tech-boom winners IPO'd within the prior 8 years.

## 5. Leadership & Catalysts — the best-of-breed names and the fuel

**Don't buy what you know — familiarity signals late-stage over-ownership.** *(backbone — brief)*
*Why:* a household "growth stock" is one institutions already accumulated — its margin-acceleration phase is spent AND its ownership is saturated, so any disappointment unleashes enormous supply with no marginal buyer left. The edge lives in unfamiliar names emerging from a primary base. *Overrides default:* Lynch's "buy what you know," inverted.
*Instance:* Circuit City +63,000% (1981–2000) → zero.

**Lead-the-sector: best-of-breed peaks before its industry is obviously hot; let the stock find the group.**
*Why:* institutions concentrate first into the single best name before broadening, so group-level confirmation is a LAGGING signal arriving after the leader's best entry — this inverts the standard top-down economy→sector→group→stock sequence. Early on a true leader has only one or two confirming names. Run a stock-first (bottom-up) search. *Overrides default:* waiting for sector confirmation before buying — and thereby buying the leader late or buying a laggard in a now-obvious hot group.

**>60% of superperformers ride a group advance.**
*Why:* if a clear majority of big winners come with group tailwinds, a lone strong stock in a dead group is statistically the lower-probability bet — this converts "strong group helps" from a soft preference into a weighted near-requirement, while leaving room for the minority of solo winners.

**The leader's breakdown is the group's early-warning siren.**
*Why:* the same institutional money and demand cycle that lifts a group's leader first also exits it first, so when a top-2-or-3 name breaks down AFTER a big advance it is the highest-information early tell of distribution spreading through the supply chain (suppliers/customers) — forward risk a broad index won't show yet. *Overrides default:* watching your own stock in isolation.
*Instance:* Netflix IPO'd; within 15 trading days Blockbuster's stock permanently topped.

**One-third of prior-cycle leaders round-trip; abandon them as candidates.**
*Why:* leadership is driven by a specific earnings/theme cycle that exhausts — once the institutions that front-ran it exit at the first hint of slowing growth, the crowding inverts; the base rates make a former leader statistically more likely to round-trip than repeat. *Overrides default:* recycling last cycle's winners.
*Instance:* one-third give back ALL or more of the advance (avg subsequent decline 50–70%, 80–90% post-bubble); fewer than 25% of one cycle's leaders lead the next.

**The 5% estimate-revision tripwire (symmetric).** *(spec.md has the window)*
*Why:* estimate revisions are the institutional transmission mechanism — an upward revision literally raises projected fair value in every fund's model and mechanically lowers P/E (bigger denominator), inviting buying; 5% is the threshold where the revision moves models rather than being noise. Watch the current quarter AND the current/next fiscal year vs 30 days earlier; UP ≥5% → better-than-average performance, DOWN ≥5% → worse. *Overrides default:* "rising estimates are good" is obvious; the 5% cutoff, the full-year requirement, and the 30-day lookback are the content.

**The catalyst discriminator: undiscounted-prospect AND dual (tape + numbers) confirmation.** *(catalyst taxonomy — gap re-mine)*
*Why:* price already embeds *known* catalysts, so the edge lives only in the gap between a real change in earnings power and the market's lagging recognition of it. Requiring BOTH technical (stock acting well) AND fundamental (numbers coming in strong) confirmation filters the two classic false positives — narrative-only stories with no earnings, and unconfirmed earnings with a dead tape. *Overrides default:* treating any identifiable good-news event as a tradeable catalyst.
*Instance:* the turnaround test — "Is the stock acting well? and Are the fundamentals coming in strong? You want to see both"; develop an earnings expectation and find whether it is "widely recognized and therefore discounted."

**Quantify the catalyst's contribution, not just its existence.**
*Why:* attribution turns "has a catalyst" into a testable, sizable claim — what FRACTION of the move is product-led tells you whether the engine is durable/repeatable (pipeline refreshes) rather than cyclical or cost-driven, and gives a yardstick for whether the next launch can re-fuel.
*Instance:* Apple — "73% of that 10,000%+ growth came from newly launched products" (iPod/iTunes/iPhone).

**Deregulation / positive sector change is its own catalyst class — track the second-order group cascade.** *(catalyst taxonomy — gap re-mine)*
*Why:* a regulatory/structural shift resets the competitive and demand structure for an entire group at once, and the shock propagates into adjacent groups (suppliers, customers, enablers) — so one policy event seeds multiple sequential opportunities. *Overrides default:* looking for catalysts only inside the company.
*Instance:* 1990s healthcare reform boosted HMOs → created demand for cost-containment companies and software; the Iraq war / 9-11 lifted defense → flowed to electronic-parts and precision-measuring-device makers.

**Top-competitor (NASCAR-draft): a non-superior #2 or #3 can be the better trade.** *(catalyst taxonomy — gap re-mine)*
*Why:* when a leader has already run far and is consolidating, rotation and share-shift hand the relative-strength baton to the #2 — the catalyst IS the share-transfer event, and it can appear in the competitor's tape before the leader's, like a drafting racer passing at the right moment. Screen the top 2–3 by earnings/sales/margins/RS, not just #1. *Overrides default:* concentrating only on the single clear leader and dismissing #2 as inferior.
*Instance:* 1990–2000 Home Depot +3,700% vs Lowe's +1,000%; then Jan 2000–Jan 2004 Lowe's +100% while Home Depot fell >40%.

**The 4-to-8-week leadership identification window off a market low.**
*Why:* breaking to new highs while bear-market overhead supply is thickest is only possible if demand is structurally overwhelming; the fixed window tells you when to stop waiting and start committing — the names breaking to new highs within 4–8 weeks off the low ARE the leaders. *Overrides default:* "leaders emerge early" is known; the specific window/new-high screen is not.
*Instance:* PCYC/Emulex/GRA/Amgen each broke to new highs within ~9–22 trading days of the index low, then ran 90–4,100%.

**Postearnings drift is a liquidity phenomenon, not a free lunch in inefficiency.**
*Why:* the persistent post-surprise drift lasts months because large buyers physically cannot deploy at once — they must accumulate slowly to avoid running it up. Naming the mechanism as a liquidity/execution constraint (not "the market hasn't figured it out") is what makes it tradeable: a meaningful surprise gives a multi-month window of *forced* accumulation, and bigger surprises drift longer. *Overrides default:* the efficient-market claim that price instantly and fully prices a surprise.

**Your edge over institutions is liquidity/speed, not information.**
*Why:* capital size inverts the opportunity set — a manager who must move blocks is pushed toward big floats, broad diversification, and seeking "informational superiority"; the individual should compete where size *forbids* the trade (small-float, high-growth names with near-zero personal slippage), not try to out-research. *Overrides default:* "institutions have superior research, so diversify like the pros."

**The power play / high tight flag — the one setup bought WITHOUT fundamentals.** *(spec.md has the numbers)*
*Why:* velocity begets velocity — a thrust of +100% in <8 weeks on huge volume is the market discounting a major change the reported numbers haven't caught up to (approval, litigation, new product), sometimes on no news; the extreme prior strength IS the fundamental signal, so demanding earnings would mean missing the discount. VCP digestion (a tight ≤20–25% range) is still mandatory. *Overrides default:* "a buy always requires confirming fundamentals," and "a stock up 100% is too high."
*Instance:* TASR 2004 → +329% in 16 weeks.

## 6. Risk-as-setup-quality — read risk as eligibility, never as a sizing prescription

These were a risk/sizing cluster in the source; the sizing, stop, and exposure prescriptions are out of scope and dropped. What remains is the *diagnostic* core — what a risk read MEANS for whether a setup is even eligible.

**Company survival ≠ capital survival — the why-avoid-Stage-4 diagnostic.** *(backbone — brief; sizing stripped)*
*Why:* "it won't go bankrupt" is irrelevant to *your capital's* survival — even a quality blue chip can draw down 70–99% and take a decade-plus to break even, and that dead-money drawdown destroys compounding whether or not the firm thrives. This is exactly why a broken Stage-4 leader is uninvestable here regardless of "quality" — the binding constraint is price risk, not business risk. *Overrides default:* "buy quality and hold through anything."
*Instance:* Xerox topped 1973, 24 years to break even while the S&P rose 500%; Avon −86% in one year, still there 14 years later.

**Healthy-leader correction depth band: 25–35% healthy / >50% fails / 60% redline / 2–3x-market.** *(setup-quality, not a stop)*
*Why:* base depth proxies the volume of trapped longs sitting at higher prices — once overhead supply exceeds a threshold, every approach to new highs hits a wall of break-even sellers, so a deep base is structurally weaker no matter how clean it looks. The depths here are **the duration-keyed tiers of §3 read at a glance, not a flat gate** — time absorbs supply, so a short base tolerates less and a ~year-long base more (a 3-week base down 30% is broken; an 18-month base down 45% has merely washed out). As a one-line summary: a healthy leader holds ~25–35%, stretches toward ~50% only in a severe bear or a long base, and a 60%+ correction is the one near-absolute redline. The relative filter — avoid correcting >2–3x the market's own decline — is the regime-normalizer that makes the read portable across bull and bear. *Overrides default:* overhead supply is known; that the *tolerance scales with base length* (not a single number) is the content.

**Deceleration / low-P/E is a leading indicator of an earnings collapse, not a value floor — the value-trap flag.**
*Why:* price moves before reported earnings — a falling P on a still-high trailing E mechanically produces a low P/E in the window *before* E catches down; a 3–5x absolute multiple near a 52-week low is the market saying "this E is going to zero." The tell is the conjunction: low P/E AND near a price low AND deteriorating fundamentals. *Overrides default:* "P/E at the bottom of its range = margin of safety / mean-reversion buy."
*Instance:* Morgan Stanley late-2007 hit a 10-yr-low P/E, then the negative print sent P/E >120x and the stock <$7; BofA/Citi/AIG all hit 10-yr-low P/Es then fell >90% within 12 months.

**Store-rollout pace >~100 net new/yr is the saturation alarm before earnings show it.**
*Why:* rapid expansion front-loads earnings while quietly cannibalizing the comp base and overshooting site quality; opening-pace acceleration is observable in real time whereas the comp collapse shows up only later — so pace is a leading saturation indicator the income statement hasn't priced. *Overrides default:* reading aggressive expansion as a growth accelerant.
*Instance:* Starbucks opened 1,102 MORE stores in 2006 than the prior year, then −82% over 24 months.

**Same-store-sales: ~10% healthy floor, 25–30%+ unsustainable — too cold AND too hot both signal trouble.**
*Why:* comp growth has only two drivers (price, customer volume), both with ceilings — a print far above the sustainable band borrows future quarters and mean-reverts into a deceleration miss, so the actionable quantity is the *upper* bound, which naive analysts misread as strength. *Overrides default:* "higher same-store sales is strictly better."

**Valuation-based brokerage upgrade after a major break flags a SHORT, not a buy.**
*Why:* the upgrade reasons from a now-stale earnings model while disregarding the tape, but the price break is the market pricing in that model's coming downward revision — so it's systematically backward-looking exactly when the trend has turned, an inverted SELL tell. *Overrides default:* reading a reputable-house Buy on a beaten-down quality name as a bottom signal.
*Instance:* CMG on the short-alert list 8 days BEFORE Citi's Buy upgrade; ~23% gap-down in 3 days.

## 7. Execution / Convergence — does it all point the same way *now*?

Sizing, stops-as-orders, R:R, scaling, and trader self-coaching are out of scope and dropped. What remains is the convergence logic and the analytic/methodology reads that decide whether a correct *analysis* fires.

**Trade only at probability convergence — the four-way-intersection rule.** *(backbone — brief; the load-bearing frame)*
*Why:* entry is a logical AND across quasi-independent evidence streams (fundamentals, price action, volume, market regime), not a weighted average — because the factors are roughly independent, demanding simultaneous alignment *multiplies* the conditional win-probability rather than averaging it. So 3-of-4 is a no-trade, not a smaller trade; a strong fundamental story does NOT compensate for a wrong tape. *Overrides default:* netting a weak factor against a very strong one. (The "free-look" selectivity point folds in here: there is no ante for waiting, so the rational policy is extreme selectivity — bet only when the setup confirms in your favor.)

**Sell on non-confirmation, not just on loss.**
*Why:* the momentum entry thesis is a prediction about imminent movement, so the *absence* of the predicted move falsifies the thesis on the same footing as an adverse move. A stock that just sits after you buy has falsified the thesis as surely as one that drops; treating "flat" as neutral lets dead capital degrade into the danger zone. *Overrides default:* the stop-loss frame where you only act when price breaches a level.

**The single largest decline since Stage 2 began is a sell signal — even on a great beat.**
*Why:* big institutions exit in anticipation of decelerating earnings, so the largest-since-the-move break is the footprint of that exit BEFORE the deceleration is reportable. The "market must be wrong, I'll buy the dip" instinct fails because the big players are selling because they know something not yet public; magnitude relative to the stock's OWN move is the tell. *Overrides default:* treating a sharp drop on a good beat as an overreaction to buy.
*Instance:* Crocs reported a +144% YoY beat and fell 36% in one day, then −99% within a year.

**Failure-to-rally on good news is a leading tell.**
*Why:* price is the aggregate of better-informed hands — when a stock can't hold or rally on unambiguously good news (a beat or a guidance RAISE), smart money is using the good-news liquidity to distribute; the "dog that doesn't bark" front-runs deterioration that becomes public later. The DEPTH of the sell-off (breaking the prior up-leg vs shallow profit-taking) is the line between healthy and broken. *Overrides default:* reading good-news-plus-a-dip as a buying opportunity.
*Instance:* Rosetta Stone 2009 raised guidance, sold off and couldn't rally; 11 days later it lowered guidance.

**Management is your WORST information source once problems emerge.**
*Why:* structural conflict of interest — management has both the strongest incentive and the best ability to obscure deterioration, so reassurances are *negatively* correlated with reality at exactly the moments that matter, while the tape aggregates informed sellers and leads disclosure. When price and narrative diverge, weight the price. *Overrides default:* seeking reassurance from the company and trusting the most-informed insiders.
*Instance:* GM 2008, AIG, Lehman, Enron, WorldCom — "management is probably your worst source of information."

**Tennis ball vs egg — the post-breakout holdability diagnostic.**
*Why:* the speed and shape of recovery is a real-time accumulation gauge — institutions supporting a position absorb every dip quickly, so brief shallow pullbacks that snap to new highs within days (volume contracting on the pullback, expanding on the recovery) reveal demand still dominates (a "tennis ball"); pullbacks that don't spring back, or a pattern widening into wild two-way swings, are "eggs" — the supply/demand battle has turned contested. *Overrides default:* treating any post-breakout pullback as ambiguous.

**Run several small ANDed screens, never one big multi-criteria screen.** *(discovery methodology — important)*
*Why:* a monolithic AND-screen has a multiplicative false-rejection rate — the stocks closest to your ideal (sitting near several thresholds) are the most likely to be knocked out by a hair's-breadth miss on the 12th line. Decomposing into separate screens on small compatible sets, then looking for names recurring across lists, converts a brittle AND into a soft voting system where cross-list recurrence is the signal. *Overrides default:* the engineering instinct that a tighter, more-criteria screen is a better screen.

**Two-quarter rolling average over 4/6/8 quarters to read the trend.**
*Why:* single quarters are noisy (timing, one-offs), so a two-quarter rolling average across the past 4/6/8 quarters converts jagged prints into the underlying second-derivative trend you actually trade — one ragged quarter isn't disqualifying if the smoothed trend is steadily improving, and a smoothed trend turning sharply lower over several quarters is the real deceleration warning.
