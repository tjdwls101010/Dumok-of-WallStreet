# Gotchas — traps paid for in real losses

The highest-density lessons in this method were paid for in real money, and they share a shape: each is a trap you walk into *because* the surface reading looks right. Pattern-match against this list **before you finalize a thesis**, and add a line every time a call goes wrong — a skill grows by accumulating gotchas, not cleverness. Each entry: the trap → why it fools you → the rule it leaves.

These are *analysis* traps (how a thesis goes wrong), not portfolio mechanics. This skill discovers and evaluates; it doesn't size a book.

## Discovery & link traps

- **DEDUCED ≠ CONFIRMED (SSYS).** A supplier was deduced from materials physics — "only this polymer can meet that spec, so they must buy it" — and turned out to be the *wrong* vendor; the real supplier surfaced, confirmed, at a trade show. Separately, an "unnamed leading customer" was assumed to be a specific hyperscaler and simply wasn't. **Why it fools you:** a clean physics/spec deduction *feels* like a fact. **The rule:** tag every inferred link DEDUCED, treat it as a lead not a thesis, and seek the confirming disclosure (filing / press / conference) before any conviction (§1 Discovery discipline).

## Entry & mispricing traps

- **Limited-float round-trip (BULL).** A newly-listed name ran $10 → $70+ on barely ~1% of its float actually trading, then collapsed straight back to the $10 IPO price the moment shares unlocked. **Why it fools you:** the chart looks like a thesis being validated; it's pure float mechanics. **The rule:** for any name within ~6–12 months of IPO/SPAC, price tracks *tradable* float, not fundamentals — locate and date the unlock as a % of float, and never read a post-unlock drop as a fear-dip (it's mechanical and may keep going).

- **Tax-harvest timing (ETOR).** A down-YTD quality name sold off into November and looked like a fear-dip; it kept dropping because tax-loss-harvest selling persists through Nov and only clears in December. **Why it fools you:** a November dip in a good name reads as opportunity. **The rule:** a Nov drop in a down-YTD name is partly mechanical harvest selling that *isn't finished* — wait for December to call the entry, and expect down-YTD losers to be Dec/Jan re-buy candidates.

- **Data-error mispricing (VLN).** Algos and screens modeled the company off a balance sheet showing –$82M cash that came from a *ticker-collision typo*; the real figure was +$93M net cash. **Why it fools you:** "prove the math" silently assumes the reported numbers are real. **The rule:** when a name screens absurdly cheap or dear, verify the *actual* filing numbers before trusting the screen — stale, mis-tagged, or ticker-collided data is itself a mispricing, and the gap is the trade.

## Character & classification traps

- **Prototype ≠ production (VPG).** A supplier looked locked into a flagship robotics program — but it was qualified only at the *prototype/demo* stage, and the design was at risk of being replaced for mass production. **Why it fools you:** a demo win reads as a production win. **The rule:** qualification routinely dies at the mass-production cost-down; judge the supplier by where it sits *inside the customer's program* (prototype vs production-at-scale), and watch OEM/CES disclosures for a second source qualifying (§2 designed-out monitor).

- **"Safe compounder" that isn't (TSEM).** A name was filed under "steady compounder, like the big foundry" — then moved 17% in a single day. **Why it fools you:** you inherit a name's *character* from its category label instead of reading it. **The rule:** classify a name's actual cycle stage and volatility from its own evidence (§4), never from the archetype you filed it under — an unearned "safe" label is a blind spot the position can't survive.
